Popular Music as Dialogue

Music seems to be deeply rooted in humanity’s biology and cultural prehistory, although debates about why continue to this day. One possibility is that music is contextually communicative--a dialogue between voices in a shared society.

This dialogue changed enormously in the early Twentieth Century with the advent of new communication technologies. The gramophone and the radio made recordings widely available to the working class, decoupling music from the trained expertise and encoding practices of classical and traditional formats. Popular songs were accessible, featuring catchy, sing-able chorus sections. Live performance also took on different forms, with jazz musicians ‘talking’ and ‘talking back’ across the stage using improvised melodies. Hundreds of record labels and music producers worked to commodify and promote popular recordings, although industry consolidation would leave only a handful of companies to dominate the global music market by the late 1930s. Mass culture favoured repetition and familiarity; most people would recognize the top single of the day.

Household televisions further exposed people to music in the 1950s, and the international success of bands like the Beatles in the following decades marked the rise of popular musicians as celebrities. A wide array of branching genres would explode from the bedrock of American gospel, blues, and jazz: folk, country, rock, metal, punk, soul, R&B (rhythm & blues), funk, disco, reggae, and hip-hop, along with dozens of subgenres and aggressive micro-classifications. MTV’s 1981 launch prompted the need for hit songs to have an accompanying music video, as entertainment synergies saw music increasingly intertwined with television, magazines, films, and video games. Shortly thereafter, the Sony Walkman cassette player began the popularization of headphones and portable music. The best-selling recording media gradually shifted from records, to cassette tapes, to CDs.

The 1990s introduced digital equipment for recording and mixing, and the popularization of computers represented a new direction for music in electronica. The internet was a major disruption for the industry, as MP3s and file sharing piracy threatened profitability and influence. By the late 2000s, the remaining ‘big four’ labels—Universal, Sony, Warner, and EMI—controlled the vast majority of the global music market. However, the emergence of online streaming services like Spotify and Apple Music has radically changed the nature of ‘selling’ songs and albums, and user-driven sites like YouTube, Bandcamp, and SoundCloud have helped indie (independent) musicians distribute their work and build an audience without immediate corporate sponsorship.

For more than a century, popular music has been in constant dialogue: a ‘talking’ and ‘talking back’ across the boundaries of culture. Many of these conversations pertain to topics from the first half of our course—social class, gender, sexual orientation, and race—but there are other themes in popular music worth examining as well:

  • Dialogue about age: what it’s like to be young and misunderstood (see, for example, Blink-182’s "What’s My Age Again?")
  • Dialogue about time: referencing or calling back to older songs or musical eras, often nostalgically
  • Dialogue about space: mythologizing certain places as culturally important (Jay-Z’s "Empire State of Mind" is a love letter to New York City, while the cover of his Views album featured Drake sitting atop Toronto’s CN Tower)

  • Dialogue about freedom: the freedom to choose how to live and who to love; non-conformity
  • Dialogue about religion
  • Dialogue about emotional reaction to life’s experiences
  • Dialogue about commercial success vs. indie creativity
  • Dialogue about instrumental music vs. vocals
  • Dialogue about studio-produced music vs. live performance

The most overt form of dialogue is the direct response song, where artists use their music to speak directly to each other. Direct responses are often central to celebrity feuds—Taylor Swift’s "Bad Blood" is about an apparent betrayal by Katy Perry. Diss tracks in rap and hip-hop are also examples of direct response music. Direct responses aren’t always antagonistic. For almost fifty years, Weird Al Yankovic has built a career by responding to popular songs of the day with satirically re-written covers. Is it possible for music to avoid dialogue? Even avant-garde music is conversational; by thwarting expectations of what music ‘should’ be; these experiments force us to acknowledge our assumptions about music’s form and pattern. Composer John Cage’s "4’33," for example, consists of almost five minutes of absolute orchestral silence, leaving the audience to contend with the musical qualities of ambient sound and crowd-noise.
